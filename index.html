Maintaining quality

- As maintainers, it is your job to ensure quality - Quality control processes can hinder contributions - Reviews can become adversarial - Link to David's pages on review? - Critical feedback can be difficult, even off-putting for contributors - There is a balance to be struck between enabling contributors and defending the project.

Heterogeneous contributors

- Diverse contributors with very different backgrounds with common goals - I see this particularly in different tools, tech knowledge, interaction with Git/GitHub - To some extent you need to insist on common tools, but you don't want to create barriers - We get _very_ opinionated about text editors and IDEs

In The Turing Way

Very diverse community.

Generally not software engineers.

Contributions are mostly prose.

Everyone can make valuable contributions and technical skills is not a measure of a contributors worth.

- _Very_ diverse community - Educational and professional background - Age - Location - Language - Interests - Ethnicity - Lived experience - Not generally software engineers - Many have and will never build the book (and that is _fine_) - Contributions will be make using many tools, from Vim to GitHub web interface - Mostly text (inspection/review is more manual, no test suite for prose) - _All_ of these people make valuable contributions - Skill with programming does not tell us how good someone's contributions will be - Therefore we need to remove barriers and enable contributors

Why Git and GitHub anyway?

- And, why _not_ DocuWiki, MediaWiki _etc._? - The advantages of version control are _too good_ to pass on - Managing multiple contributions - History - Flexible (use whatever editor you want) - Review for _both_ quality control and lending assistance - Integrates naturally with automation and CI (checks, linting, validation) - _Do as we say_. We promote best practice and working in the open and we mean it!

Metatext

- Community Handbook - Book within a book - Contribution guide - Code of conducts - Processes - Event information - Event organising guidance - All of this is open and flexible, it can be what we need it to be

Recognising all contributions

- We value and recognise _all_ types of contributions - _Not just code_, not just prose (not just authors) - all-contributors bot used to build table - Community members are encouraged to add themselves - Crowdin API used to get translation contributions

Support

- We _work with_ our contributors - Review is an _opportunity_ to help - Culture of tagging/assigning experienced community members - This might not always be desirable. Respecting boundaries - Team members are available to help _e.g._ fixing CI, changing style

Even more support

- _Many ways_ to work directly and collaboratively with TTW community - Collaboration cafés (pomodoro, co-working, quiet working, social) - Book dashes (mini-sprints, very collaborative, pomodoro, social events, share outs) - Fireside Chats (Moderated discussions with an expert panel, facilitates debate and knowledge sharing) - Different events for different purposes or to suit different styles of working

CI and automation

- CI is the _source of truth_ - One process for everyone - No complicated instructions for contributors - No need to build - No need to test - Automate regular tasks to free people for interesting work

Lessons

Building a community takes effort

- Build it and they may not come - You need to engage with your community - What can you do to enable them? - The answer will be different for different projects. - _Which means you must …_

Know your contributors

- What kind of support is _most valuable_ to them? - The less _"technical"_ your community is, the more hands-on support they will likely need

Trust in CI

- What CI says is _the truth_ - No arguments about _"it works for me"_ - There is a _healthy level_ of quality control and you may need to iterate to find it - (Never intentionally break CI)

Version control is not optional

- The advantages are _too strong_ - Versioning/tagging - History - Bug bisecting - Managing many collaborators, out of sync contributions - It is well _worth the cost_ of on-boarding and providing support

Be flexible

- Remember that your role is to _facilitate_ contributions - Remember that you are meeting them in the middle - _Don't let perfect be the enemy of good_ - It is better to bend the rules a little or do something that "isn't my job" if it gets a good contribution merged - A spelling or grammatical mistake could even make a great first issue

But know when to be strict

  • Don't knowingly break CI.
  • Don't knowingly introduce bugs.
  • Don't introduce problems which you expect someone else to fix.
- Keeping quality high introduces some _pain_ but saves a lot of pain in the long run - Remember to be _kind and respectful_ to contributors - Even when giving _"bad news"_ - This is an opportunity to help people and make connections! - You could even codify this so that everyone knows the unbreakable rules upfront
